## 1\. Icon

The icon is the first thing users see. If it doesn't earn the tap, nothing else gets a chance. It is important to have a simple design that conveys the idea of the app.

It needs to feel polished and professional, or users will scroll past it. Customers make decisions in seconds.

Look at his **Video Templates & Audio Trends** app for creating reels. Bold color. One clear subject. I needs to read clearly on the App Store search.

It doesn't have to be a professionally designed icon, but it does need to stand out. If you're early to the App Store, don't get attached to your app icon design. Experimenting will get you more downloads.

## 2\. Screenshots

Stylize real screens from your app. Use large readable text and tell a story across the sequence.

![Image](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/HGMoJc-WIAA3PYu?format=jpg&name=large)

- **First**: Paint the picture with recognizable memes
- **Second**: The template picker tool is teased
- **Third**: The GIF video editor is shown

## 3\. Onboarding

Make your onboarding process 3–4 screens and show the outcome.

1. Do not teach how to use the app.
2. It should not be a tutorial.
3. Use one action per step.
4. Don't make the user work to understand what the app is.
5. Reinforce the messaging from the App Store screens in your onboarding.

## 4\. Paywall

If you want to monetize the app, you need this step. 75% of Viktor's revenue comes from asking for money before using the app.

If you've ever sold a paid app for $5, this is no different.

- You've already been able to get a user to download your app.
- Let's show there is value worth paying for in the first session.
- Think of your onboarding screens as a free trial into what your app can do.

**If you can't do it now, you won't be able to do it later.**

> I once shipped a paywall that only 1% of users ever saw. Do the math: 1 in 100 vs. ALL 100 customers.

If only 1% of users see your paywall and you convert at 10%, you would need 1,000 people to download your app to get one paying customer.

That's HARD!

**Here is the Gifmo paywall:**

![Image](https://pbs.twimg.com/media/HGMt0gAaUAA8JUX?format=jpg&name=large)

Gifmo's paywall.

Viktor puts the paywall right after the value moment. No hidden close button. No fake urgency. No price tricks.

Clear offer and clear value.

The App Store keeps changing rules, and you may not be able to do the "Free trial" toggle shown above. But you can leverage some of the tactics here:

- Experiment with a 3-day, 7-day, or 30-day free trial
- Weekly pricing: $5.99 - $7.99/week
- Annual pricing: $29.99 - $59.99/year
- Skip lifetime deals to drive recurring revenue

We're not making an app you would pay for; we're making an app that solves a problem the user is searching for.

If you price it based on your preferences, you will leave money on the table.

**Let's break down the paywall above:**

- Annual pricing highlights the 90% discount ($312.34 vs $29.99)
- Weekly math is calculated: $5.99/week vs. $0.58/week
- App Store rating is shown for trust and social proof

These are all known sales tactics that work. If you want to sell something you need to use comparison, multiple offers, and easy numbers.

Don't make a user think twice about your pricing. If they do, you've already lost the sale.

## 5\. Simple User Flow

One screen. One action. Create a clear path forward.

Every **extra tap** is a chance to lose the user. Every decision they have to make is a chance for them to bounce. Remove anything that makes them stop and think.

This is why Viktor's apps feel obvious. He's already cut the clutter.

## Focus on these 5 Key Points:

1. **Icon:** bold, clear, readable at thumbnail size
2. **Screenshots:** story sequence, value in position one
3. **Onboarding:** 3 to 4 screens or videos, paint the outcome
4. **Paywall:** right after onboarding, simple math
5. **Flow:** one screen, one action, clear path

If you want to monetize your app, find the value, tell a story, and improve your packaging.
